Chapter 22

Video Generation with Transformers

Understanding video — the subject of Chapter 20 — means watching a video and answering questions about it. Generating video means creating one from scratch: producing frames that are spatially coherent, temporally consistent, physically plausible, and faithful to a text prompt, all at once. In 2021 the best open models produced blurry 64×64 clips that lasted two seconds. By early 2026, open-source 14B-parameter systems generate cinematic 4K video with synchronized audio, and an 11B model trained for $200K matches the quality of proprietary frontier systems. This chapter traces how transformers replaced UNets as the backbone of video diffusion, examines the architectural innovations that made this possible — 3D VAEs, multimodal dual-stream attention, noise-level mixture of experts, asymmetric parameter allocation — and shows how controllable generation is closing the gap between raw synthesis and practical creative tools.

The Video Generation Problem

Generating a single realistic image is already difficult. Generating video compounds every challenge with a temporal dimension. Four requirements must be satisfied simultaneously. Spatial quality: each frame must look realistic, with sharp textures, correct lighting, and coherent objects. Temporal consistency: the same person must have the same face, the same clothes, the same hairstyle across hundreds of frames — any flicker or identity drift is immediately noticeable. Physical plausibility: a ball must follow a parabolic trajectory, water must splash when it hits a surface, a person must not walk through a wall. Prompt fidelity: the generated video must match the user's description, not just in content but in style, mood, and composition.

Training data presents its own challenges. WebVid (~10M video-text pairs) and Panda-70M are noisy internet-scraped datasets. OpenVid provides higher quality with curated captions. Frontier models train on proprietary datasets of curated film and stock footage, often with synthetic captions generated by vision-language models. Evaluation is equally hard: automated metrics like FVD (Fréchet Video Distance) correlate weakly with human perception of quality, and human evaluation is expensive and subjective. VBench emerged as a comprehensive benchmark decomposing video quality into 16 fine-grained dimensions.

Early Transformer-Based Video Generation

Before diffusion transformers, video generation relied on autoregressive transformers operating in compressed latent spaces.

VideoGPT

VideoGPT (2021) followed the GPT paradigm: train a VQ-VAE to compress video frames into discrete tokens, then train a GPT-style transformer to autoregressively predict the next token. The VQ-VAE converts each frame into a grid of codebook indices; the transformer learns the joint distribution over these indices across space and time. The approach worked but suffered from the fundamental limitation of autoregressive generation: errors compound frame-by-frame, and quality degraded rapidly over longer sequences. Resolution was limited to 64×64.

CogVideo and Phenaki

CogVideo (2022) built on a pretrained text-to-image model (CogView2) and added temporal attention modules to extend generation from images to video. The key insight was transfer: rather than training a video model from scratch, fine-tune an image generator and let it learn temporal dynamics. Phenaki took a different approach to duration: it used a transformer conditioned on a sequence of text prompts to generate variable-length video, with each prompt describing a different scene or action. This enabled multi-scene storytelling but temporal consistency remained limited.

All early approaches shared a common limitation: autoregressive generation in token space produced artifacts at token boundaries and struggled with the long-range temporal coherence that video demands. The solution came from diffusion models — specifically, from replacing their UNet backbone with transformers.

Diffusion Transformers for Video

The key architectural shift in video generation was replacing the UNet with a transformer as the denoising backbone in latent diffusion models. This section traces that transition from DiT to the multimodal dual-stream architectures that power today's frontier models.

DiT: The Foundation

DiT (Peebles and Xie, 2022) demonstrated that a standard vision transformer, with minor modifications, could serve as the denoising network in a latent diffusion model. The input is a noisy latent $z_t$, patchified into tokens; the output is the predicted noise $\epsilon_\theta(z_t, t, c)$ or the predicted clean sample. The critical modification is adaptive layer normalization (AdaLN-Zero), which conditions each transformer layer on the diffusion timestep $t$ and the text embedding $c$ by modulating the layer norm parameters:

$$\text{AdaLN}(h, t, c) = \gamma(t, c) \odot \frac{h - \mu}{\sigma} + \beta(t, c)$$

where $\gamma(t, c)$ and $\beta(t, c)$ are scale and shift parameters predicted from the timestep and conditioning embeddings (element-wise product $\odot$ between the learned scale vector $\gamma \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and the normalized hidden state $h \in \mathbb{R}^d$), and $\mu, \sigma$ are the layer norm statistics. DiT showed that transformer-based diffusion models scale better than UNets: doubling parameters consistently improved sample quality, following a clear scaling law.

MMDiT: Dual-Stream Multimodal Design

The Multimodal Diffusion Transformer (MMDiT), introduced in Stable Diffusion 3 and FLUX, became the foundation for video generation architectures. The key design: instead of injecting text conditioning through cross-attention (as in UNet diffusion), MMDiT processes text and visual tokens as two separate streams that interact through joint self-attention. Both modalities are projected into a shared embedding space, concatenated into a single sequence, and processed by full self-attention — enabling bidirectional information flow between text and image.

For video, the dual-stream pattern is extended with a third axis: temporal. Models like Open-Sora 2.0 use a hybrid of dual-stream blocks (where text and video features are processed with separate MLPs but shared attention) and single-stream blocks (where the modalities are fully fused). This provides the flexibility of modality-specific processing with the expressiveness of joint reasoning.

The following code demonstrates a DiT block with AdaLN-Zero conditioning.


import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class DiTBlock(nn.Module):
    """Diffusion Transformer block with AdaLN-Zero conditioning."""
    def __init__(self, d_model=1024, n_heads=16):
        super().__init__()
        self.norm1 = nn.LayerNorm(d_model, elementwise_affine=False)
        self.attn = nn.MultiheadAttention(d_model, n_heads, batch_first=True)
        self.norm2 = nn.LayerNorm(d_model, elementwise_affine=False)
        self.ffn = nn.Sequential(nn.Linear(d_model, 4 * d_model), nn.GELU(),
                                 nn.Linear(4 * d_model, d_model))
        # AdaLN: predict scale, shift, gate for both attn and ffn (6 vectors)
        self.adaLN = nn.Sequential(nn.SiLU(), nn.Linear(d_model, 6 * d_model))

    def forward(self, x, cond):
        # x: (batch, n_tokens, d_model) — noisy latent patches
        # cond: (batch, d_model) — timestep + text conditioning vector
        gamma1, beta1, alpha1, gamma2, beta2, alpha2 = self.adaLN(cond).chunk(6, dim=-1)
        # Modulated self-attention
        h = gamma1.unsqueeze(1) * self.norm1(x) + beta1.unsqueeze(1)  # (B, N, d) element-wise
        attn_out, _ = self.attn(h, h, h)                              # (B, N, d)
        x = x + alpha1.unsqueeze(1) * attn_out                        # gated residual
        # Modulated FFN
        h = gamma2.unsqueeze(1) * self.norm2(x) + beta2.unsqueeze(1)
        x = x + alpha2.unsqueeze(1) * self.ffn(h)
        return x  # (batch, n_tokens, d_model)

Sora and the Frontier of Video Generation

In February 2024, OpenAI revealed Sora — a DiT-based video generation model that produced videos of unprecedented temporal coherence and visual quality. Sora demonstrated that scaling a diffusion transformer on high-quality video data could produce a system that appeared to understand the physical world.

Spacetime Patch Tokenization

Sora's key innovation was treating video as a collection of spacetime patches. Rather than fixing the resolution and duration at training time, Sora operates on variable-size grids of spacetime patches — analogous to how language models process variable-length token sequences. A 1080p 10-second video and a 720p 30-second video are both valid inputs; the model simply processes more or fewer patches. This flexibility eliminates the need for separate models at different resolutions and enables training on diverse aspect ratios and durations simultaneously.

OpenAI framed Sora as a "world simulator" — a system that learns physical dynamics from video data. While the framing was aspirational (Sora still produces physically impossible results in many cases), it established the research direction: video generation models are not just learning to produce pretty frames, they are learning compressed representations of how the physical world behaves.

Sora 2: Multimodal Generation

Sora 2, released in September 2025, extended the architecture with a multimodal MM-DiT that processes text, images, and audio together. Separate transformer streams handle each modality, each optimized for its data type, with joint attention enabling cross-modal reasoning. The model uses learned modulation to dynamically adjust how much it relies on textual prompts versus visual cues at each denoising step. The result: 10-25 second videos with synchronized audio — dialogue, sound effects, and ambient noise generated coherently with the visual content. Sora 2 also improved physical realism: if a basketball misses the hoop, the ball rebounds realistically off the backboard, demonstrating improved understanding of cause-and-effect dynamics.

The Open-Source Video Generation Ecosystem

Within 18 months of Sora's announcement, open-source models closed the quality gap dramatically. The key enablers were not just architectural insights but efficient training pipelines, high-quality data curation, and novel parameter-efficiency techniques like mixture of experts.

Open-Sora 2.0

Open-Sora 2.0 (March 2025) is an 11B-parameter model that matches frontier quality for a training cost of $200K — orders of magnitude less than the estimated cost of proprietary models. The architecture uses a FLUX-inspired MMDiT with dual-stream blocks (text and video features processed with separate MLPs but shared attention) and single-stream blocks (fully fused). A three-stage training pipeline progressively increases resolution: (1) text-to-video at low resolution, (2) image-to-video at low resolution, (3) fine-tuning at high resolution. On VBench, Open-Sora 2.0 narrows the gap with Sora from 4.52% (Open-Sora 1.2) to 0.69%.

HunyuanVideo 1.5

HunyuanVideo 1.5 (Tencent, November 2025) achieves state-of-the-art visual quality with 8.3B parameters. Its 3D causal VAE compresses video 16× spatially and 4× temporally, producing a compact latent representation that the diffusion transformer can process efficiently. The causal design ensures that encoding is sequential in time — each latent depends only on current and past frames — enabling streaming generation and progressive refinement.

Wan 2.2: Mixture of Experts for Video

Wan 2.2 (Alibaba, July 2025) introduced the first open-source Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture for video generation. The insight is that different phases of the denoising process require different capabilities: early steps (high noise) need to establish overall layout, composition, and scene structure; late steps (low noise) need to refine fine details, textures, and sharpness. Wan 2.2 uses a two-expert design where a router directs tokens to a high-noise expert or a low-noise expert based on the current denoising timestep. The total parameter count is 27B, but only 14B are active per denoising step — reducing compute by 50% while maintaining or exceeding the quality of dense models. Wan 2.2 achieved SOTA among all open and closed models on several benchmarks, and is released under Apache 2.0.

Mochi 1: Asymmetric Design

Mochi 1 (Genmo) introduced the Asymmetric Diffusion Transformer (AsymmDiT) — a 10B-parameter model where the visual processing stream has nearly 4× more parameters than the text stream. The reasoning: generating video requires far more computational capacity than understanding a text prompt, so parameter allocation should reflect this asymmetry. Mochi jointly attends to text and visual tokens over a context window of 44,520 tokens using 3D RoPE positional embeddings. Its companion AsymmVAE achieves 128× total compression (8×8 spatial, 6× temporal) into a 12-channel latent space — among the most aggressive compression ratios in video generation.

LTX-Video: Real-Time Generation

LTX-Video (Lightricks) achieved a different milestone: real-time video generation. On an NVIDIA H100, LTX-Video generates 5 seconds of 24fps video at 768×512 resolution in 2 seconds — faster than the video's playback duration. The successor, LTX-2, adds simultaneous audio and video generation and supports up to 4K resolution at 50fps. Both are released under Apache 2.0. The speed comes from an efficient latent space design and optimized attention patterns, demonstrating that quality and speed are not inherently opposed.

Architectural Components for Video Generation

Every video generation model shares a common pipeline: compress video to a latent space (3D VAE), denoise in that space (diffusion transformer), and decompress back to pixels (VAE decoder). This section examines the key architectural components that differ across models.

3D Causal VAE

The 3D causal VAE jointly compresses spatial and temporal dimensions. HunyuanVideo's VAE compresses 16× spatially and 4× temporally — a 5-second 720p video (120 frames × 720 × 1280 × 3) becomes a latent tensor of roughly 30 × 45 × 80 × 16 channels. The "causal" design uses causal 3D convolutions: each latent depends only on current and past frames, never future ones. This enables autoregressive-compatible encoding and streaming generation.

Recent work (CVPR 2025) identified a limitation of standard causal convolutions: the unidirectional information flow prevents inter-frame interaction, causing inconsistent quality across frames. The proposed fix — group causal convolution (GCConv) — uses standard convolution within each temporal group of frames and causal padding only between groups. This balances temporal coherence with causal compatibility and enables higher temporal compression ratios (8×-16×) needed for longer video generation.

3D RoPE: Rotary Embeddings in Space and Time

Standard RoPE encodes 1D position in a sequence. For video, position has three dimensions: time $t$, height $h$, and width $w$. 3D RoPE extends rotary embeddings by assigning separate frequency bands to each dimension. The position encoding for a token at $(t, h, w)$ applies rotary transformations along each axis independently:

$$\text{RoPE}_{3D}(q, t, h, w) = R(t, \Theta_t)\, R(h, \Theta_h)\, R(w, \Theta_w)\, q$$

where each $R(\cdot, \Theta)$ is a block-diagonal rotation matrix (matrix multiply, $R \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$, applied sequentially to the query vector $q \in \mathbb{R}^d$), and $\Theta_t, \Theta_h, \Theta_w$ are frequency parameters for each dimension. Mochi 1 uses 3D RoPE over 44,520 tokens, enabling the model to distinguish spatial and temporal positions without learned absolute embeddings. The factored design means the model generalizes to resolutions and durations not seen during training — a critical property for flexible generation.

Asymmetric Architectures

Mochi 1's AsymmDiT demonstrated that symmetric parameter allocation between modalities is wasteful. Generating video requires far more computational capacity than understanding a short text prompt. In AsymmDiT, the visual stream has a larger hidden dimension than the text stream, giving it approximately 4× more parameters. The modalities still share attention (using non-square QKV projections to unify the different hidden dimensions), but the visual branch can learn more complex representations. The companion AsymmVAE uses a large encoder and a small decoder — encoding quality matters more than decoding speed — achieving 128× compression while maintaining reconstruction fidelity.

MoE Routing by Noise Level

Wan 2.2's noise-level MoE is a video-generation-specific innovation. In standard MoE, a router assigns each token to an expert based on the token's content. In Wan 2.2, the routing decision is based on the denoising timestep: at high noise levels (early denoising), the layout expert handles coarse structure — scene composition, object placement, camera angle. At low noise levels (late denoising), the detail expert refines textures, lighting, and fine-grained motion. The total model has 27B parameters but only 14B are active per step, achieving the quality of a dense 27B model at the cost of a 14B model.

Classifier-Free Guidance in Video

Classifier-free guidance (CFG) controls the trade-off between sample quality and diversity. During training, the text conditioning is randomly dropped with some probability, teaching the model to generate both conditionally and unconditionally. At inference, the model produces two predictions — one conditioned on the text prompt, one unconditional — and the guided output amplifies the difference:

$$\hat{\epsilon}_\theta = \epsilon_\theta(z_t, \varnothing) + s \cdot (\epsilon_\theta(z_t, c) - \epsilon_\theta(z_t, \varnothing))$$

where $\epsilon_\theta(z_t, c)$ is the conditional prediction, $\epsilon_\theta(z_t, \varnothing)$ is the unconditional prediction, $s > 1$ is the guidance scale (scalar multiplication of the difference vector, element-wise subtraction between two noise predictions, both $\in \mathbb{R}^{C \times T \times H \times W}$), and the result $\hat{\epsilon}_\theta$ is the guided noise estimate. For video, CFG can be extended beyond text: motion guidance conditions on optical flow, and camera guidance conditions on camera trajectory parameters, enabling fine-grained control over dynamics.

The following code demonstrates classifier-free guidance for a video diffusion model.


import torch

def classifier_free_guidance(model, z_t, timestep, text_embed, guidance_scale=7.5):
    """Apply classifier-free guidance to a video diffusion model."""
    # z_t: (batch, channels, T, H, W) — noisy latent video
    # text_embed: (batch, seq_len, dim) — encoded text prompt
    # Conditional prediction: noise given text
    noise_cond = model(z_t, timestep, text_embed)    # (batch, C, T, H, W)
    # Unconditional prediction: noise without text (null conditioning)
    null_embed = torch.zeros_like(text_embed)
    noise_uncond = model(z_t, timestep, null_embed)  # (batch, C, T, H, W)
    # Guided prediction: amplify the difference
    noise_guided = noise_uncond + guidance_scale * (noise_cond - noise_uncond)
    return noise_guided  # (batch, C, T, H, W) — use for one denoising step
Key Insight

The architectural innovations in video generation — MoE routing by noise level, asymmetric parameter allocation, 3D causal VAE, 3D RoPE — share a common principle: exploit the structure of the problem to allocate computation where it matters. Early denoising needs different expertise than late denoising (MoE). Visual generation needs more capacity than text understanding (AsymmDiT). Temporal compression should be causal for streaming (causal VAE). Position should factorize across space and time (3D RoPE). Each innovation maps a structural insight about video onto an architectural design choice.

Controllable Generation and Video Editing

Raw text-to-video generation produces a video that matches a prompt, but creative applications require finer control: specific camera movements, precise object positions, reference images as starting frames, or editing specific regions of an existing video. This section covers the techniques that bridge generation and practical creative tools.

Video ControlNet

Video ControlNet extends the ControlNet paradigm from images to video. A conditioning signal — a sequence of depth maps, edge maps, pose skeletons, or segmentation masks — is injected into the diffusion transformer through a lightweight parallel branch. The conditioning operates per-frame, with temporal coherence maintained by the base model's attention mechanism. Models like Wan 2.1 Fun ControlNet accept depth, Canny edge, or OpenPose control sequences, enabling structure-preserving video generation from reference videos.

Camera Control and Motion Prompting

EPiC (Efficient and Precise Camera Control) achieves precise camera trajectory control with less than 1% additional parameters. It constructs "anchor videos" — synthetic reference videos with the desired camera motion — and uses a lightweight Anchor-ControlNet module to inject camera guidance into the diffusion process. The anchor video provides visible-region guidance that the model uses to infer the full 3D camera trajectory.

Motion prompting (CVPR 2025) takes a different approach: instead of specifying camera trajectories, the user draws motion trajectories on the image — arrows showing how objects should move — and the model generates video that follows those motion paths. This provides intuitive, direct control over dynamics without requiring knowledge of camera parameters or 3D geometry.

Image-to-Video Generation

Stable Video Diffusion fine-tunes a pretrained image diffusion model by adding temporal attention layers between existing spatial attention layers. The first frame is provided as conditioning; the model generates subsequent frames that maintain consistency with the initial image. This "inflate-and-fine-tune" approach reuses the image model's spatial quality while learning temporal dynamics from video data. Most modern models (Open-Sora 2.0, HunyuanVideo 1.5, Wan 2.2) natively support both text-to-video and image-to-video through unified training.

Video Inpainting and Editing

VideoPainter (2025) enables any-length video inpainting and editing through a dual-stream architecture. A context encoder preserves background information from unmasked regions, while the generation stream produces foreground content in the masked area. The dual-stream design maintains background fidelity — the background does not degrade or shift during editing — while generating temporally consistent new content in the edited region.

TokenFlow propagates edits temporally through feature-space attention. Given a single edited frame, TokenFlow identifies corresponding features across other frames using attention maps from the original video, then transfers the edit to all frames simultaneously. Because the propagation happens in feature space rather than pixel space, the edit naturally adapts to perspective changes, occlusions, and lighting variations across the video.

The following code demonstrates a simple video ControlNet conditioning injection.


import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class VideoControlNetBlock(nn.Module):
    """Lightweight ControlNet conditioning for video diffusion."""
    def __init__(self, d_model=1024, d_cond=256):
        super().__init__()
        # Project conditioning signal (e.g., depth maps) to model dimension
        self.cond_encoder = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Conv3d(1, d_cond, kernel_size=3, padding=1),  # single-channel depth input
            nn.SiLU(),
            nn.Conv3d(d_cond, d_cond, kernel_size=3, padding=1),
        )
        # Zero-initialized projection to base model dimension
        self.zero_proj = nn.Linear(d_cond, d_model)
        nn.init.zeros_(self.zero_proj.weight)  # start with zero influence
        nn.init.zeros_(self.zero_proj.bias)

    def forward(self, base_features, control_signal):
        # base_features: (batch, n_tokens, d_model) — from base DiT
        # control_signal: (batch, 1, T, H, W) — e.g., depth map sequence
        B, _, T, H, W = control_signal.shape
        cond = self.cond_encoder(control_signal)                # (B, d_cond, T, H, W)
        cond = cond.flatten(2).transpose(1, 2)                  # (B, T*H*W, d_cond)
        cond = self.zero_proj(cond)                             # (B, T*H*W, d_model)
        return base_features + cond  # additive conditioning — zero-init means no effect at start
Evaluation Challenge

Video generation evaluation remains an unsolved problem. FVD (Fréchet Video Distance) correlates poorly with human preference. VBench decomposes quality into 16 dimensions (temporal consistency, motion smoothness, aesthetic quality, etc.) but still misses physical plausibility and narrative coherence. Human evaluation is expensive and subjective. The gap between automated metrics and perceived quality is one reason the field advances faster than our ability to measure it.

Video generation has moved from a research curiosity to a practical creative tool in under three years. The next chapter steps back from individual architectures to examine how vision transformers — for understanding, detection, segmentation, and generation alike — are trained: the data regimes, augmentation strategies, and optimization recipes that determine whether a model's potential is realized.
