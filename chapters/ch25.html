Chapter 25

Speech Recognition Transformers

In 2016, building a production speech recognizer meant assembling a Rube Goldberg machine of Gaussian mixture models, hidden Markov states, a phonetic lexicon, and a hand-tuned n-gram language model — a pipeline with six moving parts that had to be rebuilt from scratch for every new language. A decade later, a single 2-billion-parameter transformer transcribes fourteen languages at 5.42% word error rate using nothing but supervised cross-entropy, and a separate 7-billion-parameter model does the same for more than 1,600 languages, hundreds of which had never been represented in any ASR system before. By the end of this chapter you will understand how self-supervised learning broke the labelled-data bottleneck, how Whisper made large-scale weak supervision the default, how the Conformer and its descendants rebuilt the encoder from scratch, how the transducer quietly took over streaming, and how the speech-LLM paradigm dissolved the boundary between "ASR model" and "multimodal assistant" to push every modern leaderboard under 6% WER.

Self-Supervised Foundations: wav2vec 2.0 and HuBERT

Labelled speech is expensive. Annotating one hour of conversational audio costs roughly ten hours of transcriber time, and the result is usually tied to a single dialect, a single microphone, and a single recording environment. Before 2020, scaling past a few thousand hours of paid labels was economically prohibitive for any language other than English. The field needed a way to learn from raw audio — the petabytes of podcasts, audiobooks, YouTube soundtracks, and radio archives sitting unannotated on the open web.

The breakthrough was to transplant BERT-style masked prediction into the acoustic domain. The trick is that speech has no discrete tokens to mask. A raw waveform is a continuous signal sampled at 16 kHz, and neighbouring samples are almost perfectly correlated. You cannot simply "hide" one timestep and ask the model to predict it — the answer is trivially close to the neighbour. wav2vec 2.0 solved this by first collapsing the waveform into coarse frames with a convolutional feature extractor, then masking entire frames inside a transformer encoder, and finally training the model to distinguish the true masked frame from distractor frames drawn from elsewhere in the same utterance.

The contrastive loss for a single masked position $t$ takes the form

$$\mathcal{L}_t \;=\; -\log \frac{\exp\!\big(\operatorname{sim}(c_t, q_t)\, /\, \kappa\big)}{\sum_{\tilde q \in \{q_t\} \cup \mathcal{D}_t} \exp\!\big(\operatorname{sim}(c_t, \tilde q)\, /\, \kappa\big)}$$

where $\operatorname{sim}(a, b) = a^\top b / (\|a\|\|b\|)$ is a cosine similarity (dot product of normalized vectors, yields a scalar), $\mathcal{D}_t$ is a set of $K \approx 100$ distractor quantized frames drawn uniformly from other positions in the same utterance, and $\kappa$ is a learned temperature. The numerator rewards the model for identifying the correct masked quantized target; the denominator keeps the representation sharp by forcing it to separate from everything nearby.

The surprising result from wav2vec 2.0 was the labelled-data efficiency after pretraining. Fine-tuning the 300M-parameter wav2vec 2.0 Large encoder with a Connectionist Temporal Classification head on ten minutes of labelled LibriSpeech reached 4.8/8.2% WER on the clean/other test splits — numbers that previously required hundreds of hours of paid transcription. Self-supervision had broken the labelled-data bottleneck.

HuBERT (Hsu et al., 2021) replaced the contrastive objective with a cleaner BERT-style target. Instead of constructing positives and distractors on-the-fly, HuBERT runs offline k-means clustering on either MFCC features or the hidden states of a previous HuBERT iteration, assigns each frame a discrete cluster ID, and then trains the transformer to predict the cluster ID of each masked frame with a plain cross-entropy loss. Two or three iterations of "cluster, train, recluster" are enough to match wav2vec 2.0 on LibriSpeech while removing the fiddly negative-sampling machinery.

HuBERT Large (~317M parameters) was also pretrained on 60,000 hours of unlabeled audio from Libri-Light — two orders of magnitude more unlabeled speech than the LibriSpeech 960-hour supervised set. At the time, that recipe set the state of the art for low-resource ASR when fine-tuned on small labelled slices: the iterative clustering story trades a little engineering complexity for wav2vec-class accuracy without contrastive negative sampling.

Both models share the core insight that would anchor every subsequent ASR foundation model: train the encoder first on unlabelled audio, then slot in whatever decoder head matches the downstream task. Four years later, Meta's Omnilingual ASR would scale the wav2vec 2.0 encoder recipe to 7 billion parameters and 1,600 languages with exactly the same two-stage template.

Whisper: Supervised at Scale

While the wav2vec line was chasing labelled-data efficiency, OpenAI's Whisper (Radford et al., 2022) went in the opposite direction: what happens if you stop worrying about efficiency and just pour an enormous amount of weakly-labelled audio into a vanilla encoder-decoder transformer? The answer turned out to be "you get a robust multilingual ASR system that works on almost anything."

Whisper large-v3 (public release November 2023, alongside the openai-whisper package update) refined the public checkpoints: 128 mel bins on the input (versus 80 in the original paper's default recipe), additional languages such as Cantonese, stronger timestamp alignment, and engineering focus on reducing the "hallucinated transcript on silence" failure mode. Figure 25.6 places this family on the 2023 timeline, before the Turbo speedups of 2024.

Whisper's training set was 680,000 hours of audio-transcript pairs scraped from the public internet, filtered only lightly for quality. The original paper breaks this down as roughly 438,000 hours of English ASR, 117,000 hours of non-English audio with native-language transcripts across 96 languages, and 125,000 hours of non-English audio paired with English translations (X→en). Those are hours, not language counts: the decoder still selects among 99 language tags at inference time (Figure 25.2), which is why you will see both "96" and "99" in Whisper discussions. Instead of treating the buckets as separate tasks, Whisper folded them into a single sequence-to-sequence objective by prepending task-specifying tokens to the decoder output.

The input side follows the paper's recipe: every 30-second clip is turned into a log-mel spectrogram with 80 mel bins, 25 ms windows, and a 10 ms hop, producing a fixed tensor of shape $80 \times 3000$ for the original checkpoints. large-v3 uses 128 bins instead — the OpenAI whisper package reads model.dims.n_mels so you never hard-code the wrong count. A two-layer Conv1D stem halves the frame rate, then a 32-layer transformer encoder processes the sequence without any causal masking — the encoder always sees the whole utterance. The decoder is a standard causal transformer that attends to the encoder via cross-attention and generates text tokens one at a time.

The elegant move is the decoder prompt. Every sample begins with a fixed four-token prefix: <|SOT|>, a language tag, a task tag (<|transcribe|> or <|translate|>), and either timestamp tokens or <|notimestamps|>. At inference time you simply build the prefix you want and let the model generate from there. One checkpoint, four behaviours.

Below is a two-part walk-through: first the mel filterbank in bare PyTorch (so the linear algebra is visible), then the official openai/whisper implementation loading large-v3-turbo — all torch.nn modules, no HuggingFace transformers wrappers. The library's log_mel_spectrogram uses n_mels=model.dims.n_mels so large-v3's 128-bin input matches the checkpoint.

import math, torch
import whisper

SAMPLE_RATE, N_FFT, HOP, N_MELS = 16_000, 400, 160, 80

def mel_filterbank(n_mels=N_MELS, n_fft=N_FFT, sr=SAMPLE_RATE):
    # Convert Hz → mel (the perceptual frequency axis)
    def hz_to_mel(f): return 2595.0 * math.log10(1.0 + f / 700.0)
    def mel_to_hz(m): return 700.0 * (10.0 ** (m / 2595.0) - 1.0)
    mels = torch.linspace(hz_to_mel(0), hz_to_mel(sr / 2), n_mels + 2)
    hz   = mel_to_hz(mels)
    bins = torch.floor((n_fft + 1) * hz / sr).long()        # FFT bin indices
    fb = torch.zeros(n_mels, n_fft // 2 + 1)
    for m in range(1, n_mels + 1):
        lo, c, hi = bins[m - 1], bins[m], bins[m + 1]
        fb[m - 1, lo:c]  = (torch.arange(lo, c) - lo).float() / max(c - lo, 1)
        fb[m - 1, c:hi]  = (hi - torch.arange(c, hi)).float() / max(hi - c, 1)
    return fb                                                # (n_mels, n_fft/2+1)

def log_mel_spectrogram(waveform):                           # (T,) in [-1, 1]
    window = torch.hann_window(N_FFT)
    stft   = torch.stft(waveform, N_FFT, HOP, window=window, return_complex=True)
    power  = stft.abs() ** 2                                 # (freq, frames)
    mels   = mel_filterbank() @ power                        # matrix multiply: (n_mels, freq) × (freq, frames)
    log_mels = torch.clamp(mels, min=1e-10).log10()
    log_mels = torch.maximum(log_mels, log_mels.max() - 8.0) # Whisper dynamic-range clip
    return (log_mels + 4.0) / 4.0                            # rough normalization, (n_mels, frames)

# OpenAI Whisper: load_model + pad_or_trim + mel with correct n_mels for the checkpoint
model = whisper.load_model("large-v3-turbo").eval()
audio = torch.randn(30 * SAMPLE_RATE)                        # placeholder waveform
audio = whisper.pad_or_trim(audio)
mel = whisper.log_mel_spectrogram(audio, n_mels=model.dims.n_mels).unsqueeze(0).to(model.device)
# mel shape: (1, n_mels, 3000) — 128 mels for large-v3 family, 80 for tiny–large-v2

with torch.no_grad():
    enc = model.encoder(mel.half())                          # (1, T_enc, 1280) for large
print("encoder output shape:", tuple(enc.shape))

Full decoding uses whisper.decode(model, mel, whisper.DecodingOptions(...)), which runs the decoder loop and BPE vocabulary internally. The important architectural point matches Figure 25.2: set DecodingOptions(task="translate") instead of "transcribe" when you want speech-to-English translation from the same checkpoint.

In October 2024 OpenAI released Whisper large-v3 Turbo, which kept the 32-layer encoder but shrank the decoder from 32 to 4 layers and dropped translation data from fine-tuning. The result runs at roughly 216 times real-time on a single H100 while losing less than one percentage point of WER against the full large-v3. Turbo is a perfect case study in where ASR compute actually goes: the encoder is called once per clip, but the decoder is called once per generated token, so shrinking the decoder is pure latency savings. Outside OpenAI, the CMU WAVLab OWSM v3.1 / v4 project reproduced the entire Whisper recipe on the open ESPnet toolkit using only publicly available data, releasing every training script and checkpoint — the first fully transparent alternative to the closed Whisper release.

Conformer and Its Successors: Rebuilding the Encoder

Whisper's encoder is a plain transformer. That works, but it leaves speed and accuracy on the table, because speech has a very particular structure that vanilla self-attention is a poor match for. Acoustic events — the burst of a stop consonant, the formants of a vowel — unfold over windows of 20–40 ms, which maps to just two or three frames at the 50 Hz frame rate used by most ASR encoders. Pure attention can learn these local patterns, but convolutions model them almost for free.

The Conformer (Gulati et al., 2020) made that observation concrete. Each Conformer block sandwiches a self-attention module and a depth-wise separable convolution module between two "half-step" feed-forward networks, in the pattern FFN → MHSA → Conv → FFN that the authors called a Macaron layer. The convolution captures local acoustic context; attention captures global dependencies; the two halves of the Macaron smooth the gradient flow around both. Conformer took over the LibriSpeech leaderboard within a year of its release and is still the encoder used inside Google's Universal Speech Model and inside every IBM Granite Speech checkpoint.

By 2024 the Conformer itself was being refined. FastConformer (NVIDIA) increased the input stride from 4× to 8× so that long-form audio costs four times less attention memory, and replaced the expensive pointwise convolutions with depth-wise separable variants. Streaming Conformer variants (used in production stacks and long-form ASR) pair causal depthwise convolutions with chunked causal self-attention — each frame only attends inside a sliding window of past frames — so latency stays bounded while the stack still benefits from the Conformer block's local–global split. E-Branchformer (Kim et al., 2022) replaced the sequential attention-then-convolution flow with parallel branches that are merged by a learned gating module, which simplified the residual structure. And the current champion, Zipformer (Yao et al., ICLR 2024), threw out the uniform-depth assumption entirely.

The Zipformer insight is that not every transformer layer needs to see every frame. A stack of blocks in the middle of the encoder can downsample the sequence by a factor of two or four, process the coarser representation in a wider model, and then upsample back for the final layers — a U-Net in time. Because attention is quadratic in sequence length, running six blocks at a 12.5 Hz frame rate is dramatically cheaper than running six blocks at 50 Hz, and the bottleneck forces the model to compress context into higher-level features. Zipformer also reuses attention weights across consecutive blocks inside each stack (each block pays for one attention computation but gets two applications of it) and replaces LayerNorm with a simpler "BiasNorm" that adds a learned bias before scaling, which the authors found more stable at small batch sizes. The overall result is an encoder that beats Conformer on WER while using fewer parameters and less memory.

Here is a minimal Conformer block in pure PyTorch, stripped to the essentials. A real implementation would add dropout, LayerNorm everywhere, and pre-norm residuals; the point of the snippet is to show how attention and convolution are stitched together inside one block.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class ConformerBlock(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d_model=256, n_heads=4, conv_kernel=31, ffn_mult=4):
        super().__init__()
        self.ffn1 = nn.Sequential(
            nn.LayerNorm(d_model),
            nn.Linear(d_model, d_model * ffn_mult), nn.SiLU(),
            nn.Linear(d_model * ffn_mult, d_model))
        self.mhsa_norm = nn.LayerNorm(d_model)
        self.mhsa = nn.MultiheadAttention(d_model, n_heads, batch_first=True)
        # Depth-wise separable convolution over the time axis
        self.conv_norm   = nn.LayerNorm(d_model)
        self.pointwise_1 = nn.Conv1d(d_model, 2 * d_model, kernel_size=1)  # GLU expansion
        self.depthwise   = nn.Conv1d(d_model, d_model, kernel_size=conv_kernel,
                                      padding=conv_kernel // 2, groups=d_model)
        self.pointwise_2 = nn.Conv1d(d_model, d_model, kernel_size=1)
        self.ffn2 = nn.Sequential(
            nn.LayerNorm(d_model),
            nn.Linear(d_model, d_model * ffn_mult), nn.SiLU(),
            nn.Linear(d_model * ffn_mult, d_model))
        self.final_norm = nn.LayerNorm(d_model)

    def forward(self, x):                        # x: (batch, seq_len, d_model)
        x = x + 0.5 * self.ffn1(x)                # Macaron half-step
        y = self.mhsa_norm(x)
        y, _ = self.mhsa(y, y, y, need_weights=False)
        x = x + y
        # Convolution branch: (B, L, D) → (B, D, L) for Conv1d, then back
        y = self.conv_norm(x).transpose(1, 2)
        y = torch.nn.functional.glu(self.pointwise_1(y), dim=1)  # splits channels in half
        y = self.depthwise(y)
        y = torch.nn.functional.silu(y)
        y = self.pointwise_2(y).transpose(1, 2)
        x = x + y
        x = x + 0.5 * self.ffn2(x)                # Macaron half-step
        return self.final_norm(x)

block = ConformerBlock()
frames = torch.randn(2, 250, 256)                 # batch=2, 5 s at 50 Hz, d_model=256
print(block(frames).shape)                        # torch.Size([2, 250, 256])

Two details deserve attention. First, the convolution branch uses a GLU expansion (pointwise_1 produces $2d$ channels and the GLU halves them back to $d$ via an element-wise gate), which gives the block a cheap multiplicative interaction between neighbouring time steps. Second, the two feed-forward blocks are scaled by $\tfrac{1}{2}$ — the "Macaron" coefficient — so that each block contributes a half residual, which empirically stabilizes training when you stack 16+ of them.

Decoding Strategies: CTC, Attention, and Transducers

An ASR encoder produces a sequence of frame representations. A decoder has to turn that frame sequence into a much shorter sequence of characters or sub-word tokens. The mismatch — sixty encoder frames per second versus maybe twelve characters per second — is the core alignment problem, and different decoders make very different trade-offs against it.

Connectionist Temporal Classification (Graves et al., 2006) is the simplest option. CTC adds a single extra "blank" symbol to the vocabulary and lets every encoder frame emit any token — including blank — independently. A post-processing step collapses runs of identical tokens and then removes blanks, so the emission sequence h h ∅ e ∅ l l ∅ l o collapses to hello. The training objective marginalizes over all emission paths that collapse to the correct target using a forward-backward dynamic program, identical in structure to the HMM forward algorithm:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{CTC}} \;=\; -\log \sum_{\pi \in \mathcal{A}(y)} \prod_{t=1}^T p(\pi_t \mid x)$$

where $\mathcal{A}(y)$ is the set of frame-level alignments that collapse to the target $y$ and each frame prediction $p(\pi_t \mid x)$ is produced by a per-frame softmax. The crucial property is conditional independence: the model assumes every frame is predicted independently given the encoder state, which makes CTC trivially parallel at training time and perfectly streamable at inference time. The cost is that CTC cannot model output dependencies — it has no way to know that after emitting Q it should expect u.

An attention-based encoder-decoder (AED) takes the opposite stance. The decoder is a causal transformer that auto-regressively generates tokens, cross-attending to the encoder at every step. That gives it full output-side context and almost always produces lower WER than CTC on offline data, but it is awkward for streaming (the decoder cannot start until the encoder has processed the entire utterance) and it can hallucinate confidently under distribution shift because the language-model prior inside the decoder overrides the acoustic signal.

The RNN-Transducer (Graves, 2012) splits the difference. It has three pieces: an encoder that produces acoustic frames, a prediction network that operates on the partial output sequence like a small causal language model, and a joint network that combines the two at every (frame, token) position. Decoding walks a 2-D lattice: at each step you either emit the blank symbol and advance in time, or emit a real token and advance in the output sequence without consuming a frame. RNN-T streams like CTC, but because it conditions each new token on the previous output tokens via the prediction network, it models output dependencies like AED.

The Token-and-Duration Transducer (TDT, Xu et al., 2023) is the most recent innovation in this line. TDT keeps the RNN-T structure but has the joint network emit two numbers at each step: a token distribution and a duration distribution, where the duration says "skip this many frames before asking me again". The model learns to spend one step per token for short sounds and several frames per token for long vowels, which cuts the number of decoder calls by roughly 2× with no WER loss. NVIDIA's Parakeet-TDT-0.6B-v3 is built on exactly this design and currently has the highest multilingual throughput on the HuggingFace Open ASR Leaderboard.

The code below implements greedy CTC decoding — the simplest possible inference path. It takes a batch of log-probabilities from a CTC-trained encoder and applies the collapse-then-remove-blanks rule.

import torch

BLANK_ID = 0      # convention: blank token sits at index 0

def ctc_greedy_decode(log_probs, id_to_char):
    """log_probs: (batch, frames, vocab)   -- output of a CTC head"""
    best_path = log_probs.argmax(dim=-1)                # (batch, frames)
    decoded = []
    for row in best_path.tolist():                      # iterate over batch
        collapsed = []
        prev = None
        for tok in row:
            if tok != prev:                              # 1) collapse runs
                collapsed.append(tok)
            prev = tok
        text = "".join(id_to_char[t] for t in collapsed  # 2) drop blanks
                       if t != BLANK_ID)
        decoded.append(text)
    return decoded

# Tiny demo: fake a 12-frame emission that should decode to "cat"
vocab = "_abcdefghijklmnopqrstuvwxyz"     # index 0 = blank "_"
id_to_char = list(vocab)
T, V = 12, len(vocab)
logits = torch.full((1, T, V), -20.0)
# Emit: _ c c _ _ a a _ t t _ _
path = [0, 3, 3, 0, 0, 1, 1, 0, 20, 20, 0, 0]
for t, c in enumerate(path):
    logits[0, t, c] = 0.0
print(ctc_greedy_decode(logits.log_softmax(-1), id_to_char))  # ['cat']

Hybrid CTC/attention training gives a shared encoder two heads: a CTC linear layer for streaming alignment and an attention decoder for quality. The loss is typically a weighted sum $\lambda \mathcal{L}_{\text{CTC}} + (1-\lambda) \mathcal{L}_{\text{att}}$. At inference, beam search can fuse a CTC prefix score with the attention score so partial hypotheses stay streaming-friendly while the attention path recovers word-level context. The pattern is standard in ESPnet and similar toolkits.

Real systems replace the argmax with a beam search that fuses an external language model into the CTC prefix score, which recovers a chunk of the WER gap against AED without giving up the streaming property. For applications that can afford a larger decoder, RNN-T and TDT remain the default in production (Google Assistant, YouTube live captions, NVIDIA Riva); for the state of the art on offline benchmarks, the next section shows what replaced all of them.

Distillation and On-Device ASR

Once Whisper worked, the next question was whether it had to be so expensive. Whisper large-v3 is 1.55 billion parameters and needs roughly 10 GB of GPU memory for real-time inference — perfectly reasonable on a datacentre GPU, totally impossible on a phone. Two parallel efforts attacked that gap.

Distil-Whisper (Gandhi et al., 2023) applied large-scale pseudo-label distillation. The authors ran Whisper large-v2 over 22,000 hours of open-source audio, kept only the outputs whose WER against any existing ground truth fell below a threshold (the WER heuristic filter), and then trained a student model with the Whisper encoder frozen and the decoder shrunk from 32 to 2 layers. The result is 5.8× faster inference at 49% fewer parameters while staying within 1% WER of the teacher on out-of-distribution test sets. Distil-Whisper also turned out to be less prone to long-form hallucination than Whisper itself, because the filtered pseudo-labels never included the garbled transcriptions that cause the teacher to loop on silence.

Moonshine (Moonshine AI, 2024) took a different route: train a new encoder-decoder from scratch with architectural choices tuned for edge devices. The model uses rotary position embeddings instead of absolute ones, which lets it handle arbitrary-length audio without re-training, and it is trained on audio segments of varying length without any zero padding, which means inference cost scales linearly with clip length instead of being pinned to Whisper's 30-second window. Moonshine Tiny matches Whisper tiny.en's WER while using a fifth of the compute per second of audio. Moonshine v2 (2025) added an ergodic streaming encoder — self-attention restricted to a sliding window whose size is bounded — to shrink the time-to-first-token on live transcription to sub-100-ms latencies, which is what makes real-time voice commands feel instantaneous on a watch.

These compression efforts live in a three-way trade-off between model size (parameters), runtime cost (latency / throughput), and transcription quality (WER). The diagram below lets you slide a target size and see how each family of models positions itself.

Two more practical details matter for deployment. Apple's WhisperKit showed that a quantized Whisper model plus a handful of Metal kernels can serve billion-parameter ASR on the Neural Engine with no cloud round-trip, at the cost of burning through the device's sustained compute budget. And every on-device system has to solve chunked streaming and endpointing: you need to chop the incoming audio into overlapping windows, run the encoder on each chunk with a small look-ahead, and stitch the decoder state across boundaries without re-emitting already-printed tokens — then decide when the speaker has stopped (voice-activity + silence duration) so you do not cut off trailing words or flush the buffer too early. That last piece is often harder than the model itself.

Massively Multilingual Speech Foundation Models

The 99 language tags in Whisper's decoder felt like a lot in 2022. Four years later that scale is table stakes, and the frontier has moved from hundreds to thousands of languages. The pressure came from two directions: researchers who wanted to serve the long tail of languages that have never had ASR at all, and product teams who wanted one model that could replace the entire legacy per-language Kaldi stack.

Google's Universal Speech Model (USM, 2023) was the first serious push past 100 languages. USM is a 2-billion-parameter Conformer encoder trained in three stages: self-supervised on 12 million hours of unlabelled YouTube audio spanning 300+ languages, then text-injected with 28 billion sentences to align the acoustic representation with a textual vocabulary, then fine-tuned on a smaller labelled set. It powers the automatic captions on YouTube for languages ranging from Amharic to Cebuano, and its FLEURS WER was 65% relatively lower than Whisper's at the time of release.

Meta's SeamlessM4T v2 (2023) pushed the same idea into speech translation. The UnitY2 architecture wraps a speech encoder with both a text decoder (for ASR and speech-to-text translation) and an acoustic-unit decoder (for speech-to-speech translation), then pipes the units through a PRETSSEL vocoder. The Seamless follow-up added two more models — SeamlessExpressive preserves speaking rate, pauses, and voice timbre across the translation, and SeamlessStreaming uses a wait-k policy to emit partial translations before the speaker has finished — which together make live cross-lingual conversation practical.

NVIDIA took the "high-quality over broad coverage" approach. The Canary-1B-v2 and Parakeet-TDT-0.6B-v3 models (2025) are built on a FastConformer encoder paired with either a transformer decoder (Canary) or a TDT head (Parakeet), both trained on the 1-million-hour Granary corpus. Granary covers 25 European languages and was curated with the explicit goal of high-quality audio-transcript alignment. The trade-off is narrower language coverage in exchange for near-perfect accuracy on the ones it does cover — Parakeet-TDT currently sits at the top of the multilingual-throughput category of the Open ASR Leaderboard.

The most dramatic jump came in November 2025 with Meta's Omnilingual ASR. The system scales the wav2vec 2.0 encoder to 7 billion parameters and trains two decoder variants: one CTC head for efficiency and one LLM-style transformer decoder for accuracy. The training set is 120,710 hours of labelled speech spanning 1,690 languages, of which more than 350 had never been represented in any ASR system before. The headline result is that 78% of the supported languages now fall under a 10% character error rate, and — the most important property for a long-tail system — new languages can be added with just a handful of paired (audio, transcript) examples, because the encoder has already learned a nearly-universal acoustic representation.

The philosophical shift in this section is worth naming. Between 2022 and 2025 the field stopped treating ASR as a per-language engineering project and started treating it as an emergent property of a large enough audio foundation model. You no longer train a system for Amharic; you fine-tune a 7B encoder with twenty minutes of Amharic audio and it already works.

Speech-LLM Fusion: The Post-Whisper Paradigm

If the 2020–2023 story was "how do we train good acoustic encoders," the 2024–2026 story is "how do we reuse the LLMs we already have." Text language models already know grammar, facts, rare words, and how to follow instructions. Retraining all of that inside a speech-only decoder is enormously wasteful. The natural question is: can we bolt an audio encoder onto an existing LLM and have it do ASR?

The pattern that emerged is as simple as it sounds. An audio encoder (typically a FastConformer or scaled wav2vec 2.0) produces frame embeddings; a small modality adapter — a linear projection, a Q-Former, or a windowed Q-Former — maps those embeddings into the text LLM's input space at a much lower rate; and a frozen or LoRA-adapted text LLM takes over as the decoder. The LLM sees the adapted audio tokens exactly as if they were prompt tokens, then generates text.

NVIDIA's SALM (Chen et al., 2023) was the first proof of concept: a 110M NeMo audio encoder, a linear adapter, and a 2B Megatron LLM, with LoRA rank-16 adapters on the LLM's attention layers as the only trainable weights. SALM showed that the LLM could follow ASR instructions in-context — you could paste a list of rare medical terms into the text prompt and watch recall on those terms jump by several percentage points without any fine-tuning.

Alibaba's Qwen2-Audio line (2024) established audio–text chat and audio understanding on top of the Qwen LLM family before the team shipped a dedicated ASR checkpoint. Qwen3-ASR-1.7B (Jan 2026) dropped the LoRA step entirely for that ASR path: it uses a full Qwen3 backbone, end-to-end trained with audio prompts interleaved with text, and supports 52 languages plus dialects, timestamp prediction, and in-context language identification all out of the same checkpoint. NVIDIA's Canary-Qwen 2.5B currently holds 5.63% average WER on the English Open ASR Leaderboard with exactly the same architecture family.

From there the field scaled on the enterprise side. IBM Granite Speech 3.3 (2025) uses a Conformer encoder, a windowed Q-Former that temporally downsamples the audio embeddings before feeding them into the LLM (so that a 20-minute clip fits in context on a single H100), and LoRA adapters on top of Granite 3.3 Instruct. Granite Speech 3.3 8B is near the top of the Open ASR Leaderboard and — crucially — it can transcribe arbitrarily long audio, because the LLM decoder has a large context window while the audio encoder only sees a streaming chunk at a time.

As of the April 2026 leaderboard snapshot, the top spot belongs to Cohere Transcribe (March 2026). Cohere's design inverts the usual parameter budget: more than 90% of its 2B parameters live inside a FastConformer encoder, with only a lightweight transformer decoder on top. The insight is that at inference time, the encoder is called once per clip but the decoder is called once per generated token, so spending compute on the encoder is cheaper in real-time latency per unit of quality than spending it on the decoder. Cohere Transcribe was trained on 500,000 hours of curated audio-transcript pairs with synthetic noise augmentation from 0 to 30 dB SNR, supports 14 enterprise-critical languages, and sits at 5.42% average WER — the current number to beat.

The code sketch below shows the SALM-style wiring in pure PyTorch. The encoder and LLM are both frozen; only the adapter and the LoRA parameters on the LLM attention are trainable.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class WindowedQFormer(nn.Module):
    """Downsample audio frames to a short prompt via learned query vectors."""
    def __init__(self, d_audio=1024, d_text=4096, n_queries=32, n_heads=8):
        super().__init__()
        self.queries = nn.Parameter(torch.randn(n_queries, d_audio) * 0.02)
        self.cross   = nn.MultiheadAttention(d_audio, n_heads, batch_first=True)
        self.proj    = nn.Linear(d_audio, d_text)                 # into LLM embedding space

    def forward(self, audio_frames):                              # (B, L_audio, d_audio)
        B = audio_frames.size(0)
        q = self.queries.unsqueeze(0).expand(B, -1, -1)            # (B, n_queries, d_audio)
        out, _ = self.cross(q, audio_frames, audio_frames, need_weights=False)
        return self.proj(out)                                      # (B, n_queries, d_text)

class SpeechLLM(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, audio_encoder, text_llm, adapter):
        super().__init__()
        self.audio_encoder = audio_encoder.requires_grad_(False)  # frozen
        self.text_llm      = text_llm.requires_grad_(False)       # frozen (LoRA added elsewhere)
        self.adapter       = adapter                              # trainable

    def forward(self, waveform, text_prompt_ids):
        frames = self.audio_encoder(waveform)                     # (B, L, d_audio)
        audio_tokens = self.adapter(frames)                       # (B, n_queries, d_text)
        text_embeds  = self.text_llm.embed_tokens(text_prompt_ids)
        # Concatenate audio "tokens" in front of the text prompt
        inputs_embeds = torch.cat([audio_tokens, text_embeds], dim=1)
        return self.text_llm(inputs_embeds=inputs_embeds).logits  # (B, L_out, vocab)

# --- shape smoke test with dummies ---
encoder = nn.Linear(1, 1024)          # placeholder audio encoder
llm     = type("LM", (nn.Module,), {
    "embed_tokens": nn.Embedding(32_000, 4096),
    "forward": lambda self, inputs_embeds: type("Out", (), {"logits": inputs_embeds @ torch.randn(4096, 32_000)})(),
})()
adapter = WindowedQFormer()
model   = SpeechLLM(encoder, llm, adapter)

audio = torch.randn(2, 500, 1)        # (B, frames, 1) — pretend frames
prompt = torch.randint(0, 32_000, (2, 16))
# This sketch is intentionally schematic; plug in a real FastConformer + Qwen to run it.
print("adapter is the only trainable module:",
      sum(p.numel() for p in model.parameters() if p.requires_grad))

What do you actually gain from putting an LLM behind the encoder? Four things. Rare-word recall — the LLM's pretraining already contains "Nakamura" and "serotonin reuptake," so the ASR head does not have to learn them. In-context biasing — list your product names in the system prompt and see them recognized on the first pass. Instruction following — "transcribe only the questions," "output in JSON with speaker tags," "translate Spanish parts into English" all work out of the same checkpoint. And joint tasks — ASR + summarization + translation compose naturally because everything downstream of the adapter is just text.

Key Insight

From 2020 to 2023 the rule was "ASR is about the encoder." From 2024 onward the rule is "ASR is about the decoder being a large language model that happens to accept audio in its prompt." That single reframing is why 2024–2026 leaderboards show a clean break: everything above 5.9% WER on English Open ASR is a Whisper-style model, and everything below is a speech-LLM.

Evaluation and the Open ASR Leaderboard

Every model above is judged against the same small collection of benchmarks: LibriSpeech (clean and other), TED-LIUM, GigaSpeech, VoxPopuli, FLEURS (multilingual, n-way parallel speech), AMI (meeting audio), CommonVoice, and Earnings-22 (financial call transcripts). The two metrics that matter are Word Error Rate — the edit distance between hypothesis and reference divided by the reference length — and Character Error Rate for non-segmenting languages like Chinese, Japanese, and Thai where word boundaries are arbitrary.

The subtlety is that WER is extremely sensitive to normalization. Punctuation, casing, number formatting, filler words ("uh", "like"), and contractions can swing the number by several percent, so every leaderboard pipes the hypotheses through a Whisper-style text normalizer before scoring. Even then, WER flatters models that happen to share the leaderboard's normalization conventions — a model trained to output "one hundred" will look worse than one that outputs "100" on a reference that writes "100", regardless of which transcription the listener would actually prefer.

Two things are striking about the April 2026 snapshot. First, the top four entries are within half a percentage point of each other, which strongly suggests the benchmark itself is approaching saturation and the remaining gap is noise in the evaluation set rather than a real model capability gap. Second, every model in the top four is a speech-LLM; the transducer-only Parakeet-TDT just barely cracks the 6% ceiling and the encoder-decoder Whisper family is a full 1.5–2 points behind. The era of "just a speech model" is over.

Finally, WER is not the only thing worth measuring. The axes that the leaderboard does not capture — and that the chapter on Evaluating Audio and Speech Models covers in depth — are the ones that bite in production: hallucination on silence or music (Whisper is notorious for emitting "thanks for watching" on empty clips), long-form temporal drift where timestamps desynchronize over multi-hour files, code-switching where the speaker changes language mid-sentence, named-entity accuracy for proper nouns that rarely appear in training data, and speaker-overlap robustness in meetings and debates. Those are the failure modes a 5.42% average WER does not tell you about, and they are where the next generation of benchmarks is going.

Caveat

Leaderboard numbers are snapshots. The English Open ASR Leaderboard is updated weekly and a 5.42% WER as of April 2026 will almost certainly not be the top entry by the time you read this chapter — the question to ask when a new model appears is not "what is its WER" but "what new capability (language, latency, controllability) did it unlock at that WER." Quality is saturating; differentiation is moving to the axes WER cannot see.
